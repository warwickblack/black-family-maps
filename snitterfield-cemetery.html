1. The Trevelyan Family

Headstone Reference

IMG_1391.CR2 — Inscribed "George Otto Trevelyan — Skilled painter of the landscapes"

Key finding: The headstone commemorates Caroline, Lady Trevelyan (née Philips, 1849–1928), wife of Sir George Otto Trevelyan. The Snitterfield parish newsletter Snipe (Issue 9) explicitly confirms: "Caroline, Lady Trevelyan, is buried in Snitterfield cemetery and her skill as a landscape painter is mentioned on her tombstone." Her husband’s grave is at Wallington, Northumberland.

Sir George Otto Trevelyan, 2nd Baronet (1838–1928)

Born 20 July 1838 at Rothley Temple, Leicestershire. Educated Harrow and Trinity College, Cambridge (Classical Tripos, 1861). A Liberal MP for over 30 years, he served twice as Secretary for Scotland under Gladstone, as Chief Secretary for Ireland (1882–84), and as Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster. A prolific author, his works include The Life and Letters of Lord Macaulay (1876) and a four-volume History of the American Revolution (1899–1905). He was appointed to the Order of Merit in 1911. Died 17 August 1928 at Wallington, Northumberland, aged 90.

Through his wife, he owned Welcombe House near Stratford-upon-Avon (approximately 2 miles from Snitterfield), which passed to him on the death of his father-in-law Robert Needham Philips in 1890. He entertained President Theodore Roosevelt there in 1910.

Caroline, Lady Trevelyan, née Philips (1849–1928)

Born 1849, daughter of Robert Needham Philips of Welcombe House. She married George Otto Trevelyan in 1869 after years of opposition from her uncle. A talented watercolourist, she painted landscapes of the family estates at Welcombe (Warwickshire) and Wallington (Northumberland), as well as scenes from her travels. A 1912 newspaper article noted "She has a high talent as a landscape painter." Died 1928 and buried at Snitterfield churchyard. Her husband died the same year and was buried at Wallington.

Children of George Otto and Caroline Trevelyan

The couple had three sons, all prominent in public life:

Sir Charles Philips Trevelyan, 3rd Baronet (1870–1958) — Liberal and later Labour politician; President of the Board of Education 1924 and 1929–31.

Robert Calverley Trevelyan (1872–1951) — Poet and classical translator; his son was Julian Trevelyan (see below).

George Macaulay Trevelyan (1876–1962) — Eminent historian; Regius Professor of History, Cambridge; Master of Trinity College.

Julian Otto Trevelyan (1910–1988)

Grandson of George Otto and Caroline. Born 20 February 1910 in Dorking. Attended Trinity College, Cambridge, then moved to Paris where he worked alongside Picasso and Miró. A celebrated Surrealist and modernist painter and printmaker, he is famous for landscape paintings of the Thames, Venice, and the Italian countryside. His studio was at Durham Wharf, London. Over 100 works are held at Tate Gallery. He died 12 July 1988. Julian was very much a child of the 1930s–40s, and the Trevelyan family’s connection to Welcombe House meant the family name was known in the Snitterfield area throughout the period 1937–1954.

Relevance to 1937–1954: Caroline died in 1928, before your father’s time in the area. However, the Trevelyan name and Welcombe House remained prominent local landmarks. The house was sold to London Midland and Scottish Railway in 1931 and converted to a hotel. Any local person growing up near Snitterfield in this era would have known the Trevelyan connection and Welcombe Hotel well.

2. The Margetts Family of Warwick

Headstones Referenced

IMG_1392, 1394, 1398, 1399, 1136 (various) — Frances Hillier Margetts (d.28 Dec 1936), Rodney Guy Wilson Margetts (d.23 Dec 1961), Mrs AL “Billie” Margetts (d.19 Oct 1976).

Family background: The Margetts family were the founders and proprietors of John Margetts & Son, auctioneers, valuers and estate agents of Warwick, established 1806. The firm still trades today (over 218 years). They are a cornerstone family of Warwickshire professional life.

William Haines Margetts (1872–1952)

Born 1872. A member of the established Warwick auctioneers’ family. He married Fanny Hillier Wilson (see below) in 1898 in Birmingham. He lived until 1952, meaning he was alive throughout your father’s time in the area (1937–1954) and would have been a known figure in local professional circles.

Frances Hillier Margetts, née Fanny Hillier Wilson (4 May 1871–28 December 1936)

Born 4 May 1871 in Milton, Glasgow/Lanarkshire, Scotland, the daughter of John Wilson (1808–deceased) and Jane Ford (1835–deceased). Her middle name “Hillier” was her mother’s family name, which she subsequently passed to several of her children. She married William Haines Margetts in 1898 and settled in Warwickshire. In 1901 they were at Knowle; by 1911 they had moved to Shrewley, near Warwick.

Fanny died 28 December 1936. Probate was granted on 23 February 1937 at the Stratford-on-Avon probate registry, with her son Rodney and a Roland Hollick as executors. She is buried at Snitterfield churchyard.

Their Five Children

1. Rodney Guy Wilson Margetts (15 April 1901, Knowle – December 1961) — Rodney’s middle name “Wilson” was taken from his mother’s maiden name. He was accepted as a Naval Cadet to the Royal Naval College, Osborne in January 1915 (listed in The Times, 26 December 1914, as one of 113 successful candidates). He later became a dairy farmer in the Stratford-on-Avon area (recorded in the 1939 National Register). He married Ada Lilian Hampton in Birmingham in 1927. He died in December 1961, aged 60, and is buried alongside his mother at Snitterfield. See Ada Lilian below.

2. Maisie Haines Margetts (2 May 1903–1 January 1973) — Named with her father’s surname “Haines” as a middle name. Married Captain Richard Glyn Griffith (1892–1944) in 1937 in Kensington, London. No children recorded. Captain Griffith died in 1944. Maisie died 1 January 1973.

3. Gladys Hillier Margetts (1904–1982) — Named with her mother’s “Hillier” name. Married William Stanley Heath. No children recorded. Died 1982.

4. Peter William Harding Margetts (31 March 1906–14 February 1985) — Married Jessie Amelia Joy (1913–1987) in 1951 at Southend-on-Sea. No children recorded. Died 14 February 1985.

5. James Morritt Margetts (1908–1971) — No spouse or children recorded in public records. Died 1971.

Mrs A.L. “Billie” Margetts (10 March 1893–19 October 1976)

This is almost certainly Ada Lilian Hampton, wife of Rodney Guy Wilson Margetts. Her initials “A.L.” stand for Ada Lilian; “Billie” was her nickname. Born 10 March 1893, she married Rodney in Birmingham in 1927. The 1939 National Register records her living in the Stratford-on-Avon area with Rodney. After Rodney’s death in 1961 she survived as his widow until 19 October 1976. A death registration for “Ada Lilian Margetts, born 10 March 1893” is recorded in England & Wales in 1976. She is buried at Snitterfield alongside her husband and mother-in-law.

Rodney and Billie’s Children

MyHeritage records indicate Rodney and Ada Lilian had at least three children, including:

Michael Rodney “Mick” Margetts (1933–2017) — Born 1933 in the Stratford area. Living in the area during your father’s time (1937–1954) — he would have been aged 4–21 during that period. He married Cecily Joy Sutherland Pope in 1954. Mick died 2017 aged 84. He had siblings including Sheila Wendy Margetts (later Martin) and Patricia Barbara Margetts.

Relevance to 1937–1954: The Margetts family were prominent professionals in Warwick and the surrounding area. William Haines (the patriarch) was alive throughout (died 1952). His son Rodney was a dairy farmer near Stratford-on-Avon. Rodney’s son Michael “Mick” was born 1933 and would have been a contemporary of young people in the area during this period. The Margetts estate agency name was on virtually every property sale in Warwickshire.

The Firm: John Margetts & Son — Corporate History, 1806 to Present

The Margetts estate agency is one of England’s longest continuously operating property firms, and its history runs in parallel with the family genealogy above. Founded by John Margetts on 31 January 1806 in Warwick, the firm was established during the Regency era — the reign of George III — at a time when the English land and property market was becoming increasingly formalised. The practice combined valuation, sale and management of rural and residential property: exactly the professional role a successful Warwickshire family would occupy as land agents and auctioneers for local estates.

The business was passed down through the Margetts family for multiple generations across the 19th and well into the 20th century. A key detail comes from Roger Wyatt, a later principal of the firm, who noted that "Bertie was the last of the Margetts family" to be directly involved. Genealogical research has now identified "Bertie" as John Bertie Palmer Margetts (born Warwick, registered 1908; christened 31 May 1908 at St Mary's Church, Warwick; died 17 September 1948, Warwickshire, probate granted 15 December 1948). He was the son of John Herbert Margetts (born 1871/72, Warwick; died 26 April 1911) and his wife Ida Mary Margetts, and had two sisters — Sybil Mary Margetts and Ivy Lesley Margetts — as recorded in the 1911 census at Warwick St Mary. John Herbert Margetts was himself a son of John William Margetts (born c.1840, Warwick), the mid-Victorian head of the firm at High Street, Warwick. John Herbert and William Haines Margetts (1872–1952), the father of Frances Hillier Margetts (buried at Snitterfield), appear to have been brothers — both sons of John William — meaning Bertie and Rodney Guy Wilson Margetts were first cousins. Bertie's early death at age 40 in 1948 ended direct family ownership, and paved the way for Roger Wyatt to join the firm shortly after (Roger, born 1940, was aged about 7 when he first met Bertie — c.1947, just a year before Bertie's death).

Genealogical connection: William Haines Margetts (1872–1952) was almost certainly a senior figure in the firm during your father’s years in the area (1937–1954). His son Rodney (1901–1961) chose dairy farming rather than the firm, and the other children (Maisie, Gladys, Peter, James) also appear not to have continued in the business. “Bertie” may therefore have been a cousin or other branch of the family rather than William’s direct children — further genealogical research could confirm this.

The Wyatt Era: Following the end of direct family involvement, Roger James Wyatt (born March 1940; BSc Agriculture, FRICS, FAAV) became the pivotal figure in the firm’s modern history. A Fellow of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors and a Fellow of the Central Association of Agricultural Valuers, Roger led or co-led the practice for a significant period. He later transitioned to the role of Consultant (formally resigning as a Director per Companies House records), suggesting a long and distinguished tenure before stepping back from executive leadership. A local account describes Roger as a well-known figure in Warwickshire land agency circles from the 1960s onwards.

Corporate incorporation (2014): Historically, the practice almost certainly operated as a sole trader or family partnership — the traditional legal structure for professional firms of this type. It was formally incorporated as Margetts Warwick Limited on 3 January 2014 (Company No. 08831060), registered in England and Wales under SIC code 68310 (Real Estate Agencies). The registered office has remained at 12 High Street, Warwick, CV34 4AP.

Current directors and leadership: The current officers listed at Companies House are:

  • Andrew Simon Goldie MRICS (born October 1965) — Active Director; a Member of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors.
  • Oliver David Thompson FNAEA FARLA CertREA CPEA CRLM (born August 1969) — Active Director; a Fellow of the National Association of Estate Agents and Fellow of the Association of Residential Letting Agents, with over 36 years in residential sales and lettings. He grew up in Kenilworth and has spent his entire career in the Warwickshire property market.
  • Roger James Wyatt — Resigned as Director; now serving as Consultant.

Services and standing: Today Margetts offers residential sales, lettings, agricultural services, RICS valuations, commercial property and buy-to-let services across Warwick, Leamington Spa and surrounding Warwickshire. The firm is regulated by RICS, NAEA and ARLA, and lists properties on Rightmove, Zoopla, OnTheMarket and PrimeLocation. It deliberately remains independent, citing this as enabling impartial advice rather than operating as part of a national corporate chain.

220+ years of unbroken history: Margetts describes itself as having an “uninterrupted history” since 1806 — making it one of the oldest continuously operating property agencies in England. As of 2026 it has been trading for approximately 220 years, surviving the Napoleonic Wars, the Victorian era, two World Wars, the Great Depression, and multiple property market cycles.

A notable parallel: The neighbouring firm Godfrey-Payton, also based in Warwick (at 25 High Street, just along from Margetts at No. 12), also claims roots back to 1806 — suggesting that both firms may have had overlapping or related origins in the same formative period of the Warwickshire professional property scene, though they have evolved as entirely separate practices.

3. The Corser Family of Snitterfield

Headstones Referenced

IMG_1395, 1396, 1397 — Harold Kenelm Corser (b.6 Jan 1900, d.19 Jul 1959), Douglas John Corser (d.28 Dec 1947, aged 77), Norma Ethel Corser (2 Jul 1910–25 Aug 2010), Camilla & Felicity Corser (stillborn twins, 25 Apr 1975).

Background

The Corser family arrived in Snitterfield in the early 20th century, coming from Bridgnorth in Shropshire. They became established as fruit farmers and later mixed farmers specialising in Hereford cattle. The Snitterfield parish newsletter Snipe (Issue 50) records: “Mr. Corser now combines his fruit farming with general farming, specialising in Hereford cattle. He grows some corn and has four tractors.”

Douglas John Corser (born 1870, Bridgnorth, Shropshire – died 28 December 1947)

Born 1870 in Bridgnorth, Shropshire, the son of Thomas Corser and Elizabeth Couser. His siblings included Thomas, William E., Archibald J., Cecil H., Alfred G., and Elizabeth Corser (1871 census). He was born into a Shropshire family; the Corser surname is of French Norman origin meaning “courser” (a swift horse), suggesting possible equestrian or agricultural lineage.

First Marriage: Douglas married Lily Wood Myers at Leamington Spa (and also in a Yorkshire church at Welton) on 26 March 1899. By the 1901 census they were living in Leamington, Warwickshire, with their first child Harold Kenelm. Lily Wood Corser (née Myers) likely died before 1905.

Second Marriage: Douglas married Eliza Alltree (born c.1886). By the 1911 census they were in Kenilworth, Warwickshire, with a daughter Dorothy Alltree Corser (born 2 June 1905 in Birmingham). Eliza Alltree Corser (née Alltree) was born c.1886 and died 1973.

Douglas died 28 December 1947 at The Hill Cottage, Snitterfield, Warwickshire, aged 77 exactly as stated on the headstone. He had a will recorded in the National Index of Wills and Administrations. He was survived by his son Harold Kenelm, his daughter Dorothy, and his second wife Eliza (who lived until 1973).

Dorothy Alltree Corser (born 2 June 1905, Birmingham)

Half-sister of Harold Kenelm, born to Douglas’s second wife Eliza Alltree. Born 2 June 1905 in Birmingham. Her name “Alltree” was taken from her mother’s maiden name — a common practice. She is noted in genealogical indices as also appearing under the married surname “Taylor.” Dorothy would have been aged 32–49 during your father’s time in the area (1937–1954) and potentially living in or near Snitterfield while her father Douglas was still alive (died 1947).

Harold Kenelm Corser (6 January 1900 – 19 July 1959)

Born 6 January 1900, the eldest child of Douglas John and Lily Wood (née Myers) Corser. Born and baptised at Leamington, Warwickshire. He served in World War I (service records exist from 1918). In the 1921 census he was in Didsbury, Manchester (aged 21). He travelled to New York in 1932. By 29 September 1939 (National Register) he was living in Matlock, Derbyshire — though he later returned to Snitterfield.

Harold married Norma Ethel (née Lang Stephenson) (born 2 July 1910). They settled in Snitterfield, where Harold continued the family fruit and cattle farming tradition established by his father. They had three children: Angela, Wendy, and Paul. Harold died 19 July 1959 aged 59. His wife Norma lived on until 25 August 2010, reaching the remarkable age of 100 — and was therefore alive in Snitterfield throughout and long after your father’s time in the area.

Harold & Norma’s Children: Angela, Wendy, and Paul Corser

The three children would have been born approximately between 1935 and 1950, placing them as exact contemporaries of your father during his years in the area (1937–1954). Specific birth years are not recorded in publicly available sources, but given Harold was born 1900 and married Norma (born 1910), their children were almost certainly born in the late 1930s to mid-1940s — making them likely schoolmates or contemporaries of anyone growing up in Snitterfield or the immediate Stratford-upon-Avon area at that time.

The headstone also records Camilla and Felicity Corser, stillborn twins who died 25 April 1975. These were almost certainly grandchildren of Harold and Norma — children of Angela, Wendy, or Paul. Their names suggest the family continued living in the area into the 1970s.

Relevance to 1937–1954: The Corser family were the most directly “local” of all the families in this burial ground during your father’s time. Douglas John Corser was a working farmer at The Hill Cottage, Snitterfield, until his death in December 1947. His son Harold was farming in the area from the early 1940s onwards. Most importantly, Harold and Norma’s children Angela, Wendy, and Paul were almost certainly born c.1938–1948 and would have been among the children growing up in Snitterfield village at exactly the same time as your father.
